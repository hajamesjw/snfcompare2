Private Equity SNF Portfolio Performance Under Scrutiny

A new academic study links private equity ownership of nursing homes to higher resident mortality, increased deficiencies, and lower staffing levels.

SC
SNF Compare Editorial
December 16, 2025

A comprehensive study published this week in the New England Journal of Medicine has reignited the national debate over private equity (PE) involvement in skilled nursing facilities. The research, conducted by a team from Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health, analyzed outcomes at 1,674 nursing homes acquired by PE firms between 2015 and 2023 and compared them to a matched cohort of non-PE facilities over the same period.

Key Findings

The study found that PE-owned facilities experienced a 10.2% increase in risk-adjusted 30-day mortality following acquisition, alongside a 16% rise in health-related survey deficiencies and a 7.5% decline in total nursing hours per resident day. These effects were most pronounced in the first 18 months following acquisition, a period often characterized by operational restructuring and cost reduction initiatives.

Perhaps most notable was the financial analysis: PE-owned facilities showed operating margins 3.8 percentage points higher than their non-PE counterparts, driven primarily by lower staffing expenditures and reduced spending on supplies and capital improvements. The researchers estimated that the aggregate cost savings across PE-owned facilities amounted to approximately $640 million annually—savings that came at a measurable cost to care quality.

Industry Response

The Private Equity Stakeholders Project praised the study as “the most rigorous evidence to date” that PE business models are fundamentally at odds with quality nursing home care. Consumer advocacy groups, including the National Consumer Voice for Quality Long-Term Care, called for immediate legislative action to restrict PE acquisitions in the healthcare sector.

Industry representatives pushed back strongly. The National Center for Assisted Living (NCAL) noted that the study did not account for the fact that PE firms often acquire distressed facilities that were already underperforming, a selection bias that could inflate the apparent negative effects of PE ownership. Several PE-backed operators pointed to their own internal data showing quality improvements at acquired facilities after an initial restructuring period.

Regulatory Implications

The study’s findings are landing at a critical moment. CMS is currently finalizing rules that would require greater disclosure of ownership structures and related-party transactions for all Medicare- and Medicaid-certified nursing homes. Several states, including California, New York, and Massachusetts, have enacted or are considering laws that require regulatory approval before PE firms can acquire nursing home operations.

For investors and operators, the growing scrutiny suggests that the regulatory environment for PE-backed SNF acquisitions will only become more restrictive. Firms that can demonstrate genuine quality improvements post-acquisition will be best positioned to navigate the evolving landscape, while those focused primarily on cost extraction may face increasing legal and reputational risk.
