5 warning signs hidden in a nursing home’s public record
These aren’t opinions — they’re documented findings from government inspectors.
Every nursing home in the United States has a public record. Fines. Complaints. Inspection failures. Staffing data. It’s all documented by CMS — and it’s all available on SNF Compare.
The problem? Most families don’t know what to look for. Today we’ll show you the 5 red flags that experienced healthcare advocates check first — and the specific thresholds that should make you think twice.

⚠ Warning Signs
5 Red Flags in a Facility’s Public Record
 
Red Flag #1
More Than 5 Health Deficiencies on the Most Recent Survey
The national average is roughly 8 deficiencies per inspection cycle, but the number alone isn’t the full story. What matters is the severity. Look for deficiencies tagged as “Immediate Jeopardy” (scope/severity level J, K, or L) — these mean a resident was in immediate danger of serious harm or death.
Threshold: >5 deficiencies AND any at the “Immediate Jeopardy” level = serious concern.
 
Red Flag #2
Substantiated Complaints in the Past 3 Years
Anyone can file a complaint, but CMS only substantiates those that investigators confirm. A substantiated complaint means a government investigator verified the problem. Multiple substantiated complaints — especially for abuse, neglect, or inadequate care — signal systemic issues, not isolated incidents.
Threshold: 2+ substantiated complaints in the past 3 years = investigate further before placing a loved one.
 
Red Flag #3
Fines or Payment Denials in the Past 3 Years
CMS doesn’t fine facilities lightly. A civil monetary penalty (CMP) means the violations were serious enough to warrant financial punishment. Payment denials are even more severe — they mean CMS has temporarily stopped paying the facility for new admissions because conditions are so poor.
Threshold: Any fine above $50,000 or any payment denial = major warning sign.
 
Red Flag #4
Staffing Rating of 1 or 2 Stars
A low staffing rating means the facility provides fewer nursing hours per resident per day than most other facilities. This directly impacts how quickly staff can respond to call lights, administer medications, and provide basic care. Studies consistently link low staffing to higher rates of falls, infections, and pressure ulcers.
Threshold: 1-star or 2-star staffing rating = residents are likely not getting enough hands-on care.
 
Red Flag #5
Declining Overall Rating Over Time
A facility that dropped from 4 stars to 2 stars is a fundamentally different place than it was a year ago. Declining ratings often follow ownership changes, staffing cuts, or management turnover — all of which directly impact daily resident care. A downward trend is far more concerning than a stable low rating.
Threshold: A drop of 2+ stars in the overall rating within the past year = something has fundamentally changed.
✓ What a safe facility looks like
✓ 4–5 star overall rating with consistent scores over multiple inspection cycles
✓ Zero substantiated complaints or documented immediate jeopardy citations
✓ No fines or payment denials in the past 3 years
✓ 3+ star staffing rating with above-average total nursing hours per resident per day
✓ Stable or improving ratings — a facility trending upward is investing in quality
